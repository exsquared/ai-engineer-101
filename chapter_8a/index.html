    How LLMs Work ‚Äî Context, Embeddings, and Reasoning

    üåâ Why Are We Here?

    In the last chapter, you built logic pipelines combining rules, models, and LLMs. But you‚Äôve probably noticed something weird:

    • Sometimes the LLM gives a brilliant answer.
    • Sometimes it sounds confident but is dead wrong.
    • Sometimes changing one word in your prompt completely changes the outcome.

    What‚Äôs going on?

    To build anything reliable with LLMs, you need to understand how they actually ‚Äúthink.‚Äù This chapter is that foundation.

    You‚Äôre not just learning how to prompt. You‚Äôre learning how the machine predicts.

    üéØ Goal

    To build a working mental model of how LLMs operate:

    • What a "language model" is ‚Äî and what it‚Äôs not
    • How inputs are processed via tokens and context windows
    • Why order, structure, and precision in prompts matter
    • What embeddings are and how they underpin many AI systems
    • When and why LLMs produce surprising or incorrect answers

    üß† What Is a Language Model?

    A language model is not a reasoning engine, chatbot, or planning agent.

    At its core, it is a system trained to do just one thing:

    Given a sequence of text, predict the most likely next token.

    That's it.

    Input: "My favorite programming language is"
Model: ["Python" (78%), "JavaScript" (10%), "C++" (5%), ...]

    Language models are trained on massive text datasets to learn:

    • Word patterns and probabilities
    • Syntax and semantics
    • How different topics and tones tend to flow

    They do not truly understand or reason. They excel at generating plausible continuations of text ‚Äî and that‚Äôs what makes them powerful (and sometimes misleading).

    üß† How Does It Predict the Next Token?

    Behind the scenes, most modern LLMs use a neural network architecture called a Transformer.

    The Transformer has a key mechanism called attention, which allows the model to look at all the tokens in the prompt and weigh their importance differently.

    Think of it like reading a paragraph with a highlighter ‚Äî the model "pays more attention" to the parts that matter most when choosing the next word.

    This allows the model to:

    • Understand long-range dependencies
    • Make predictions based on relationships between words
    • Scale to very large inputs and outputs

    üß© Tokens and Context Windows

    LLMs don‚Äôt see whole paragraphs ‚Äî they see tokens, which are fragments of text:

    • A word: dog
    • A subword: un, believ, able
    • Punctuation: . or ?

    ‚è≥ What‚Äôs a Context Window?

    Think of it like the model‚Äôs short-term memory. It‚Äôs the number of tokens it can ‚Äúsee‚Äù at one time.

    • GPT-3.5: ~4,000 tokens (~3,000 words)
    • GPT-4 (turbo): up to 128,000 tokens (~100,000 words)

    If your input + output exceeds this window:

    • Earlier context is dropped
    • The model starts forgetting what came before

    üß† Analogy: Imagine whispering into a tunnel. The model only hears the last X words you say.

    üß† Embeddings: Meaning in Vector Space

    Before LLMs predict anything, they convert text into embeddings ‚Äî high-dimensional vectors that encode meaning.

    Embeddings allow the model to:

    • Measure similarity between texts
    • Cluster related ideas
    • Match prompts to known patterns

    üß† Intent vs Prompt ‚Äî Not the Same Thing

    What you want and what you say are different.

    Example:

    ‚ùå Prompt: "Tell me about our app."

    • ü§ñ Output: vague, promotional, possibly off-topic

    ‚úÖ Prompt: "List 3 features of our app in bullet points. Include only 1 sentence per point."

    • ü§ñ Output: clear, usable, and structured

    üß± System vs User Roles

    Chat-based models use structured messages:

    messages = [
  {"role": "system", "content": "You are a precise technical assistant."},
  {"role": "user", "content": "Summarize this customer complaint."}
]
    • System message: defines tone, identity, rules
    • User message: the actual task

    ‚öôÔ∏è Temperature and Top-p: Controlling Output Behavior

    üî• Temperature

    • 0.0: deterministic
    • 1.0+: exploratory

    üé≤ Top-p

    • Select from the top p% of possible next tokens

    ‚ùó A Word on Hallucination (At the End, Where It Belongs)

    LLMs don‚Äôt ‚Äúknow‚Äù facts or have external memory. They guess what sounds right.

    That‚Äôs why:

    • They invent URLs
    • Misquote legal clauses
    • Fabricate names or facts

    How to reduce it:

    • Give more structure
    • Add documents to prompt (RAG)
    • Ask for sources, but verify

    üß† Final Analogy: A Genius Autocomplete

    Imagine a super-smart autocomplete:

    • It‚Äôs read billions of documents
    • But it only guesses what comes next ‚Äî based on what it‚Äôs seen
    • It forgets everything the moment it responds

    Powerful? Yes. Grounded? Only if you design it that way.
